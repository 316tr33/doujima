Tracing the History of Tokaido

Discover how the Tokaido has evolved from the Edo period to the present day

1601

üèõÔ∏è Birth of the Tokaido

The Tokaido was developed by Tokugawa Ieyasu, establishing 53 post towns connecting Edo and Kyoto. With the sankin-kotai system, daimyo processions traveled this route, making it Japan's most important highway bustling with merchants and travelers.

1833

üé® Hiroshige's 53 Stations of Tokaido

Utagawa Hiroshige published "The Fifty-three Stations of the Tokaido." Beautiful Tokaido landscapes including Mt. Fuji from Yui-juku and Hakone mountain paths were depicted in ukiyo-e prints, spreading the charm of the highway to common people.

1889

üöÇ Beginning of the Railway Era

The complete opening of the Tokaido Main Line brought a major shift from foot travel to railway transportation. However, the culture and history of the post towns continue to be preserved in various locations today.

What is Tokaido Walk

The Tokaido functioned as Japan's main artery from the Edo to Meiji periods. This highway established by Tokugawa Ieyasu is not merely a travel route, but a special path where culture and history breathe.

üèõÔ∏è Historical Significance

The Tokaido established by the Tokugawa shogunate in Keicho 6 (1601) is approximately 500km highway connecting Edo (present-day Tokyo) and Kyoto. By walking this historical stage where daimyo processions used for sankin-kotai, bustling post towns with merchants and travelers, and the lives of common people remain vividly, you can physically experience the lifestyle and culture of people from that era.

üé® Cultural Value

Many of the landscapes depicted in Utagawa Hiroshige's masterpiece "The Fifty-three Stations of the Tokaido" can still be experienced today. The spectacular Mt. Fuji views, remnants of post towns, and roadside pine groves depicted in ukiyo-e - the same scenery that Edo period travelers saw can be walked in modern times, making it a precious cultural heritage.

üö∂‚Äç‚ôÇÔ∏è Modern Ways to Enjoy

You don't need to walk the entire route at once. Based on your interests and physical ability, you can enjoy itineraries from one day to several days. Local gourmet food in each post town, hot springs, historic site tours, and above all, the sense of achievement and health benefits gained from walking. It's an ideal activity that combines historical exploration with health improvement.

ü§ù Travel Companionship

While Tokaido Walk can be enjoyed alone, walking with like-minded companions makes for a deeper experience. Historical discussions, local discoveries, sharing the sense of achievement in overcoming difficulties - it's also an excellent opportunity to build new relationships. In group tours, experienced guides provide more detailed historical commentary.

üóª Scenic Highway with Mt. Fuji Views

From Yui-juku to Satta Pass, this is one of Japan's most spectacular scenic routes facing Mt. Fuji directly. You can experience the same scenery as depicted in Utagawa Hiroshige's ukiyo-e "The Fifty-three Stations of the Tokaido" in modern times.
