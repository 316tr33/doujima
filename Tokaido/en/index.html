Tracing Tokaido History

Discover how the Tokaido has developed from the Edo period to the present day

1601

üèõÔ∏è Birth of the Tokaido

The Tokaido was reorganized by Tokugawa Ieyasu, establishing 53 post stations connecting Edo and Kyoto. With the sankin-kotai (alternate attendance) system, daimyo processions traveled the route, making it Japan's most important highway bustling with merchants and travelers.

1833

üé® Hiroshige's Tokaido 53 Stations

Utagawa Hiroshige published "Tokaido 53 Stations." Beautiful Tokaido landscapes including Mt. Fuji from Satta Pass and Hakone mountain paths were depicted in ukiyo-e, spreading the highway's charm among common people.

1889

üöÇ Beginning of the Railway Age

With the complete opening of the Tokaido Main Line, travel shifted from foot to rail transport. However, the culture and history of the post towns continue to be preserved in various locations today.

Representative Stations and Scenery of Tokaido

Introducing four points that encapsulate the charm of the Tokaido. You can experience the history passed down from the Edo period to the present.

Nihonbashi

Starting Point of Tokaido

Historic bridge that served as the starting point of the Five Highways. The "Road Origin Marker" installed by the Meiji government still remains today.

Hakone Station

Town of Checkpoint and Hot Springs

A special place where Edo period checkpoint ruins and modern hot spring resorts coexist. The hot springs beyond the harsh mountain paths provide exceptional healing.

Satta Pass

Between Yui and Okitsu Stations

The most famous Mt. Fuji viewing spot in Utagawa Hiroshige's ukiyo-e. The view from Satta Pass continues to inspire awe even today.

Kusatsu Station

Junction of Tokaido and Nakasendo

Kusatsu Station prospered as a transportation hub in the Edo period. The honjin (inn for feudal lords) ruins remain, an important post town conveying the prosperity of the past.

What is Tokaido Walk

From the Edo period to the Meiji era, the Tokaido functioned as Japan's main artery. This highway reorganized by Tokugawa Ieyasu is not just a route of movement, but a special journey where culture and history breathe.
