Frequently Asked Questions

To help resolve questions and concerns about the Shikoku Pilgrimage, we have compiled frequently asked questions and their answers.

The Shikoku Pilgrimage is a pilgrimage route that visits 88 temples in Shikoku associated with Kobo Daishi (Kukai). Spanning approximately 1,400km in total, this pilgrimage route has long been walked by many people as a journey to face oneself and promote spiritual growth.

You don't necessarily need to visit them in numerical order. While visiting them in sequence is called "jun-uchi," there are various methods such as "gyaku-uchi" (visiting from temple 88 in reverse order), "kugiri-uchi" (visiting in segments), and "ikkoku-mairi" (visiting only one prefecture). You can freely plan according to your physical strength and available time.

You don't need to prepare everything at the start. Begin with the minimum essentials for worship such as a stamp book (nōkyōchō), prayer beads, incense, and candles. Items like white robes and walking sticks can be purchased as you progress through the pilgrimage when you feel they're necessary. They're available at Temple #1 Ryōzenji, at each temple's shop, or through online shopping.

"Dōgyō-ninin" means "always accompanied by Kobo Daishi (Kukai)." Even when pilgrims travel alone, they believe that Kobo Daishi is watching over and supporting them from nearby. Therefore, they inscribe "Dōgyō-ninin" on their sedge hats and white robes to express their faith that they are traveling together with Kobo Daishi.

There is no strict rule, but wearing white clothing such as hakue (white robe) and sugegasa (sedge hat) helps focus your mind and makes you easily recognizable as a pilgrim. Even if you choose functional, comfortable clothing for mobility, simply wearing a wagesa (stole) around your neck can demonstrate proper respect for worship.

Yes, anyone can visit regardless of religious affiliation or sect. The 88 sacred sites of Shikoku include temples from sects other than Shingon. Many people make the pilgrimage even without religious faith, simply wanting to experience Japanese culture.
